Building a Light Display - Part 2

Soldering everything together.
By hackerman | January 19, 2022 | 3 minutes

Connecting the NeoPixels to the Raspberry Pi

This will likely be the shortest post in the series, as I do not have expertise in electronics. I also cannot solder well, and I am sure my (surprisingly many) electrical engineering friends will get a laugh. So, I will just describe the steps I took.

The Raspberry Pi outputs 3.3v and the NeoPixels or WS2811/2812 requires 5v. It is possible to get some number of LEDs to work without a 5v power supply, but it’s not the best solution. Going forward, I used the following schematic which will use the Quad Level Shifter to convert the Raspberry Pi’s 3.3v to 5v. If doing this project over, I would also add some capacitors as recommended by Neo Pixel Best Practices.

Kattni Rembor1

Required Items

  • Raspberry Pi
  • breadboard
  • 22 gauge solid core wire
  • solder and soldering iron
  • sockets (optional but handy)
  • WS12811/2 lights/neopixels
  • 5v power supply
  • 74AHCT125 Quad Level-Shifter or 74HCT245
  • double-sided tape

Assembly

I installed header pins to the Raspberry Pi. It helps with stability and give some more wiggle room for mistakes.

The breadboard used here is connected like a solderless breadboard. I attached the 74AHCT125 Quad Level-Shifter and the rest of the wiring as described in the above schematic. Going back, I would use sockets to attach the 74AHCT125 and not just the Raspberry Pi.

Installing into the bar

With some double-sided tape, I installed everything underneath the bar. Everything is pretty light and should hold fine. I will move all the power cords to the side with some clips in the near future.

The color in person is not washed out like it is in the video.

Trouble Shooting

I actually worked through putting together the hardware four times before having this final working solution. The first time I set everything up using a solderless breadboard. This worked fine, but I wanted something a little more permanent.

Physics Hard

The second attempt was soldering everything to the Raspberry Pi directly and connecting it to a smaller breadboard. I then used approximately 11 ft of 22 gauge wire to connect to the power supply. Turns out that there is not enough power getting to the NeoPixels in this fashion.

This is the start of me realizing that maybe installing sockets was a good idea.

Soldering Hard

I do not have a lot of experience soldering and ran across a few issues that caused me problems.

  • My soldering iron is in Fahrenheit. For some reason I thought it was Celsius. And I was wondering why I kept getting cold joints…
  • Kept creating cold joints, even after setting the correct temperature.
  • I’m a terrible planner – use sockets

Though I didn’t find any shorts from my shabby soldering, I do think that the cold joints caused too much resistance. Also, I’m glad I used sockets on the Raspberry Pi, wish I used it on the level shifter.

I have built a couple of working keyboards. With the way my soldering has been, I am surprised they work.
